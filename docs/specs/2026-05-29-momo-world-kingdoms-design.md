Momo’s World: Business Kingdom Game
Core idea

Momo’s World is a kid-friendly civilization exploration game where children visit magical kingdoms to understand how the real human world works through industries, business models, companies, systems, money flows, and imagination quests.

Not “business class for kids.”

More like:

Civilization + Roblox tycoon + Pokémon regions + Magic School Bus + investor pattern recognition.

Kids explore the world by asking:

“Where does food come from?”
“Why do cities need energy?”
“How do packages move?”
“Why is Apple powerful?”
“How does Netflix make money?”
“What happens if the grid breaks?”
“What kind of company would I build in this kingdom?”

The design principle

Each kingdom has two layers:

Reality Layer: how today’s industries actually work.

Imagination Layer: a magical world that makes the system emotionally memorable.

The child should leave each kingdom understanding:

what problem this industry solves
what inputs it needs
what outputs it creates
who pays whom
what makes some companies powerful
what can go wrong
what future changes may reshape it
what kind of business they could invent

Kid-Friendly Investor Logic

Do not say “moat” first. Translate it.

Moat becomes:
“What makes this castle hard to copy?”

Pricing power becomes:
“Can this company charge more because people really want it?”

Network effect becomes:
“Does it become better when more people join?”

Switching cost becomes:
“Is it hard or annoying to leave?”

Scale advantage becomes:
“Does being bigger make it cheaper or stronger?”

Brand becomes:
“Do people trust it before they even try it?”

Bottleneck becomes:
“What tiny missing thing can stop the whole kingdom?”

Capital intensity becomes:
“Does it need giant expensive machines before it can even start?”

Cyclicality becomes:
“Does this business have big sunny seasons and stormy seasons?”

Why This Can Be Special

Most kids’ business education is trapped in:

lemonade stands
basic money lessons
shallow entrepreneurship
random company facts

Momo’s World can be different because it teaches:

Civilization pattern recognition.

Kids learn:

how industries connect
how companies make money
how society functions
how technology changes systems

how imagination can redesign the world

That is rare.

Feynman Flash:
Momo’s World turns the real economy into magical kingdoms so kids can understand how food, energy, machines, cities, software, trade, schools, government, health, and the future all work together.

Meta-Cognition Snapshot:

The key mental model is “systems before labels”: kids should learn flows, dependencies, incentives, and bottlenecks before memorizing industry names.

Bias Radar:

Main trap: making it either too textbook or too fantasy. The solution is fantasy wrapper, real-world engine.

Counter-Strike:
The strongest opposing view is that this may be too ambitious and too complex for children. My answer: yes, if presented as lectures. No, if presented as exploration, missions, characters, and simple cause-effect systems.

Time-Travel Audit:

1 year success: kids can explain basic industries better than many adults.
3 year success: kids invent their own companies using real business model logic.
5 year success: Momo’s World becomes a full “civilization literacy” platform, not just a parenting app module.

Double-loop learning hook:
When kids fail a mission, ask: Does this change the model or only the tactic?

5. First-Principles Lesson

Each kingdom needs one core law.

Examples:

Earth: no raw materials, no civilization.
Energy: no power, no motion/computation/production.
Machines: tools multiply human ability.
Trade: products have no value until they reach people.
Health: humans are fragile, so trust and science matter.
Money: capital coordinates future action.
6. Kid Translation

Bad:

Good:
“The Power Kingdom is like a giant invisible river. Every home, hospital, train, and AI tower drinks from it. If too many drink at once, the river gets stressed.”

8. Mini-Course Ladder

Each kingdom needs 3 levels:

Level 1: What is it?
Concrete, object-based.

Level 2: How does it work?
Flow and system.

Level 3: Why does it matter?
Bottlenecks, money, future.

9. Quiz Bank

Each kingdom needs 30-50 quiz questions.

Not trivia. Cause-effect questions.

Example:
“Why do batteries help solar power?”
A. They make sunlight brighter
B. They store extra electricity for later
C. They replace all wires
D. They make oil cheaper
